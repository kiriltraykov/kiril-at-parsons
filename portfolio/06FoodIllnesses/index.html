V. Myths from research

Scientific research (see footnote) has tried to offer explanations that food illnesses occur more often among:

  • Lower socio-economic groups
    • Less education leads to food preparation knowledge gaps and riskier behavior (e.g. people believing that freezing food will kill bacteria)
    • Less income leads to lack of resources (e.g. cutting boards, paper towels, disinfectant soaps) to adequately prepare food in sanitary conditions
  • Ethnic minorities
    • Some cultural practices may pose risks (e.g. buying fresh Mexican cheese from independent small stores with poor sanitation)
    • Lack of English-language skills to understand proper food preparation practices

However, the theory does not match the data: the map reveals that neither poorer states (e.g. the South) nor immigrant-heavy ones (e.g. Texas, California) exhibit higher food illness rates per capita as predicted. One explanation could be that people in those income brackets and social groups may not seek medical help due to cost, unless absolutely required.

VI. Place of consumption more important

89% of illnesses have an identified location of contraction, but scientific research only focuses on those identified at home, which only account for 14% of cases. The majority of cases, 57%, originated from restaurants!


Diagram: Place of consumption, cumulative 2010-2014

"Other" includes school, nursing home, prison, camp, religious facility, fair, hospital, office, and child daycare.

Thus, to prevent food illnesses, efforts should focus on improving food handling and preparation at restaurants (the majority of cases), with more strict grading standards and practices.
